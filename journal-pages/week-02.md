## Independent study
For the independent study, I translated parts of my data portrait from Experiment 1 into an interactive p5.js sketch. Rather than recreating every detail, I focused on a few data points that could be represented clearly and meaningfully through interaction.

I developed a **Mood Flower / Mood Garden** concept, where the flower acts as a visual metaphor for my personal data. I chose this because it felt more expressive and personal than using a conventional chart.

### Data mapping
- **Energy level** = flower / petal size
- **Mood** = petal colour
- **Day category** = label and dropdown selection
- **Busyness / stress** = small dots or particles in the background

This meant that the flower could change depending on the viewer’s interaction. A higher energy level created a larger flower, while different moods changed the colour. Categories such as school, social, rest, and creative helped organise the data into different states.

I used a slider, dropdown menu, button, and checkbox as the main interactive elements. The slider represented gradual change in energy, the dropdown allowed the viewer to switch between categories, the button created a new variation or “new day,” and the checkbox allowed labels to be shown or hidden.

This interactive version revealed more than the original hand-drawn portrait because it allowed the viewer to compare different states instead of seeing only one fixed image. It made the work feel more like a system that could be explored rather than a single final drawing.

## Reflection
This experiment helped me understand that code can function as a design material rather than only a technical tool. In Experiment 1, I explored data through drawing by hand, but in this experiment I learned that p5.js can make those ideas interactive, responsive, and changeable.

The most useful thing I learned was that interaction can reveal relationships in data more clearly than a static image. A hand-drawn portrait can communicate a mood or structure, but an interactive sketch allows the viewer to test changes, compare states, and explore how different variables relate to each other.

Using AI was also an important part of the process. Because AI use was allowed, I treated it as part of my creative workflow rather than just a shortcut. It helped me generate more ambitious ideas quickly, but I still needed to test, understand, and refine the results. This made the process feel collaborative rather than automatic.

Overall, this experiment showed me that interactivity can make data visualisation feel more personal, playful, and exploratory.

---

## Next steps
Next I want to develop the visual style further and make the interactions feel smoother and more polished. If I had more time, I would improve the animation, refine the layout of the controls, and possibly allow multiple data states to be compared at once. I would also like to continue using p5.js as a creative tool for interactive storytelling and data portraiture.
